### INVESTOR DNA: Nick Sleep

#### 1. INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY

Nick Sleep’s investment philosophy is anchored in a genuinely long-term, business-focused, and benchmark-agnostic approach. The Nomad Partnership was explicitly structured as a vehicle for investors with a time horizon of five years or more, treating them as partners in a "shared destiny" rather than as passive clients. This structure was designed to cultivate patient capital, which Sleep considered a primary competitive advantage.

The core strategy evolved from "cigar butt" investing to holding a concentrated portfolio of exceptional, iconoclastic businesses for multi-year periods. The investment process ignores market indices and transient stock price quotations, focusing instead on the deep reality of the underlying business economics. Success is measured by the growth in intrinsic value of the portfolio companies over many years, not by short-term price movements.

A central tenet is the "Scale Economics Shared" model, where a business creates a virtuous cycle by passing efficiency gains and cost savings back to the customer. This builds unassailable competitive moats and drives superior long-term growth. The firm's interests are deeply aligned with its partners through a unique fee structure with clawbacks, the manager's 100% personal investment in the fund, and a commitment to never sell the management company.

#### 2. INVARIABLE TRUTHS (Market Axioms)

1.  **Price and Value Divergence:** The price and value of shares can vary greatly over time. The permanent impairment of capital is always a possible outcome.
2.  **The Virtuous Cycle of Reciprocity:** When a company consistently shares cost savings with customers through lower prices, customers reciprocate with loyalty and increased purchasing, creating a "perpetual motion machine" for retail.
3.  **The Short-Term Myopia Opportunity:** If share prices are being set by investors focused on the next data point, they cannot simultaneously be focused on long-term value, creating a structural opportunity for patient investors.
4.  **The "Anti-Bubble" Effect:** Market frenzies in one sector inevitably create an "anti-bubble" of neglected and discarded companies in other areas, presenting significant investment opportunities.
5.  **The Futility of Frequent Trading:** Investment success is achieved by holding great assets for long periods, not through frequent activity. As one saying goes, investors "can make their money sitting on their assets."
6.  **The Inevitability of Lagging:** It is a statistical certainty that any investment portfolio will, at times, lag some alternative yardstick. There will always be someone who has performed better in the short term.
7.  **The Danger of High-Low Pricing:** Variable "high-low" pricing strategies are a confusing and ultimately untrustworthy proposition for consumers, eroding the long-term loyalty that a consistent value proposition builds.

#### 3. WHAT THEY LOOK FOR (Buy Criteria)

*   **Durable Business Model:** Seeks businesses with a "Scale Economics Shared" model, where efficiency gains are passed to customers. This creates a predictable, self-funding growth engine with a low probability of failure.
*   **Expanding Competitive Moat:** Invests in companies whose competitive advantage is designed to widen over time. The ideal moat is built on a culture of low prices and customer value that rivals cannot afford to replicate.
*   **Significant Discount to Intrinsic Value:** Aims to acquire stakes in businesses trading at a deep discount, citing a target of around "half of their real business value."
*   **Owner-Oriented Management:** Favors companies run by founders or major shareholders who demonstrate intelligent capital allocation (e.g., share buybacks at low prices) and have significant personal stakes in the business.
*   **Misunderstood by the Market:** Looks for opportunities where the market misprices a company due to a short-term focus, such as penalizing a company for making long-term investments (e.g., Amazon's growth spending) or undervaluing a high-quality "cash cow."
*   **High "Robustness Ratio":** Prefers companies where customers and employees receive a large share of the value created by scale, indicating a more durable, less fragile business model.
*   **Simple and Honest Consumer Proposition:** Seeks businesses built on an unbreakable "contract" with the customer, such as a fixed, low mark-up, which builds immense trust and loyalty.

#### 4. WHAT THEY AVOID (Red Flags)

*   **Short-Term Partners:** The fund is explicitly unsuitable for investors with short time horizons or those conflicted by principal-agent issues.
*   **Rent-Seeking Business Models:** Avoids retailers that primarily profit from their suppliers (e.g., via "slotting fees") rather than by delivering value to the end consumer.
*   **Confusing Pricing Strategies:** Wary of companies that use variable "high-low" pricing, as it signals a lack of commitment to a consistent, trustworthy value proposition.
*   **Management Pandering:** A significant red flag is management "tinkering" with a successful long-term model to satisfy "quarterly EPS junkies" on Wall Street.
*   **Externally-Funded Growth:** Cautious of companies that rely on rented growth via leases for expansion, which can lead to unsustainable and undisciplined growth.
*   **Momentum & Market Frenzies:** Actively avoids participating in market manias or sectors with frenzied activity where future economics appear to be fully priced in.
*   **Closet Indexing:** Steers clear of constructing a portfolio that mimics an index while charging active management fees.

#### 5. POSITION SIZING & PORTFOLIO

The Nomad portfolio was intentionally concentrated in a relatively small number of high-conviction investments. This concentration was an accepted and communicated source of expected volatility compared to diversified peers. The Kelly criterion was used as a mental framework for position sizing, suggesting that the best ideas with the lowest probability of failure warrant a significant portfolio weighting. A key self-criticism was that a potential mistake is not buying *enough* of a truly great company. The firm sought to own a meaningful stake in its companies—enough to potentially influence outcomes—with a target in excess of 10% mentioned. The portfolio had a global, go-anywhere mandate, providing a wide opportunity set and avoiding the need to invest in unattractive geographies.

#### 6. SELL DISCIPLINE

The sell discipline is rooted in the long-term investment philosophy and is characterized more by inaction than action.

*   **Hold for Full Value:** Resists the temptation to sell winning positions, particularly in takeover situations where a quick, discounted profit is offered. The goal is to allow business value to compound and be fully realized.
*   **Thesis-Driven, Not Price-Driven:** *Inference:* Selling is dictated by a fundamental change in the long-term investment thesis—such as a deterioration of the moat, a change in management's capital allocation philosophy, or the erosion of a customer-first culture—rather than by price targets or market noise.
*   **Engagement Over Divestment:** Explicitly rejects selling shares due to ethical or political pressures. The view is that remaining a shareholder provides a vote and a platform for engagement, which is more effective for influencing change than divesting and becoming an outsider.
*   **Return Capital if Opportunities are Scarce:** Maintained the ability to return cash to partners if the investment opportunity set was deemed too small, prioritizing disciplined capital allocation over asset gathering.

#### 7. RISK MANAGEMENT

Risk is viewed primarily as the permanent impairment of capital, not short-term volatility. The entire structure of the firm and its investment process served as a risk management system.

*   **Structural Alignment:** The five-year minimum time horizon, infrequent reporting, and partner-centric communication were designed to manage behavioral risk by ensuring investors were prepared for volatility. Deferred performance fees with clawbacks ensured manager incentives were aligned with long-term results.
*   **Margin of Safety:** The primary risk mitigator was purchasing assets at a significant discount to their intrinsic business value.
*   **Business Quality Focus:** Risk was managed by selecting businesses with an extremely low probability of long-term failure, characterized by self-funded growth, durable moats, and stable models.
*   **Patience as a Shield:** The "aggregate patience of its Partners" was considered a key competitive advantage and risk mitigator, insulating the manager from pressure to react to short-term market movements.
*   **Concentration & Volatility:** The risk of high volatility from a concentrated portfolio was not avoided but was accepted, managed, and clearly communicated to partners as a necessary feature of the strategy.

#### 8. SIGNATURE PATTERNS

*   **Scale Economics Shared:** The firm's most important and frequently cited pattern. A company relentlessly returns efficiency gains to customers as lower prices, creating a virtuous cycle of higher volume, greater scale, and an ever-widening moat. (e.g., Costco, Amazon).
*   **Robustness Ratio:** An analytical framework developed to measure the fairness of how a company shares the benefits of its scale between customers, employees, and shareholders. A high ratio indicates a more durable business.
*   **Customer Reciprocation:** The observable phenomenon where customers reward a company's fair dealing and low prices with intense loyalty and higher spending, leading to superior unit economics (e.g., sales per square foot).
*   **The "Anti-Bubble" Hunt:** Actively seeking investment opportunities in companies and sectors that have been discarded and neglected during market manias focused elsewhere.
*   **The Partnership "Ecosystem":** Intentionally structuring the firm as a "Partnership" to cultivate a special, patient relationship with investors, which in turn becomes a competitive advantage. This was validated when the fund received net subscriptions during the 2008 credit crisis.
*   **Input-Focused Philosophy:** Concentrating on the inputs to investing—the hard analytical and psychological work—rather than being emotionally misled by the outputs, such as short-term price fluctuations.

#### 9. KNOWN BLIND SPOTS & MISTAKES

Nick Sleep and his partner were open about their fallibility, frequently stating, "we are human, we make mistakes, but our mistakes, we hope, are honest mistakes."

*   **Position Sizing Error:** A self-admitted mistake was potentially under-sizing the position in Costco, acknowledging that "not buying enough" of a phenomenal company is a significant portfolio error.
*   **Operational Drag:** Acknowledged a structural challenge of being tied to a larger fund management organization, causing Nomad to "move like an elephant" in the market despite its small AUM.
*   **Powerlessness of Small Stakes:** Believed it is a mistake to own a "deminimus proportion of a company," as it leaves the investor powerless to influence outcomes.
*   **Analytical Errors:** Admitted to specific errors, including a "senior moment" in mis-analyzing dilution risk and personally overpaying for an asset due to emotional reasons early in his career.

#### 11. HISTORICAL EXAMPLES

*   **Costco Wholesale:** The quintessential example of the "Scale Economics Shared" philosophy. The analysis focused on its fixed low mark-up as a "contract with the customer," the superior profitability of its maturing stores, and the customer reciprocation that drove industry-leading sales per square foot. It was described as a "perpetual motion machine trading at a discount."
*   **Amazon.com:** Another key holding, valued for its similar dedication to sharing scale benefits. Sleep championed Jeff Bezos's willingness to make decisions that were expensive in the short term (e.g., shipping subsidies) but built immense long-term value, creating an opportunity for investors who could look past quarterly earnings.
*   **Berkshire Hathaway:** Certain subsidiaries like GEICO and Nebraska Furniture Mart were cited as further examples of the "Scale Economics Shared" model in action.
*   **The Gap:** Used as a negative case study to illustrate the risks of undisciplined, lease-based expansion, which can lead to an unsustainable "purge of growth."
