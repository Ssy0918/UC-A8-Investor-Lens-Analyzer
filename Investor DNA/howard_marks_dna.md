INVESTOR DNA: Howard Marks

### 1. INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY

*   **Risk Control is Paramount:** The core of the philosophy is that superior long-term performance is built on a foundation of rigorous risk control. The primary goal is not risk avoidance but the "intelligent bearing of risk for profit," which involves ensuring the potential return adequately compensates for the risk of permanent capital loss.
*   **Risk is Permanent Loss, Not Volatility:** Marks emphatically rejects the academic definition of risk as volatility. True risk is the probability of a permanent loss of capital or an unacceptably low return. Short-term price fluctuations are not a primary concern for long-term investors with locked-up capital.
*   **Embrace Asymmetry:** The goal of superior investing is to achieve asymmetry: structuring investments to have more upside potential than downside risk. This is achieved through skill ("alpha") and by buying assets at prices well below their intrinsic value.
*   **Market Cycles & Psychology are Key:** Markets are not perfectly efficient. They are driven by pendulum-like swings in investor psychology between greed and fear (risk tolerance and risk aversion). The key is not to predict the future but to "know where we are" in the cycle and adjust the portfolio's posture—between offense and defense—accordingly.
*   **Contrarian and Unconventional:** Significant returns are not made by buying what is popular. They are made by being perceptive and unconventional, buying assets that others underestimate or are too fearful to own. This often requires taking a lonely position.
*   **Performance Profile:** The ideal outcome is to achieve market-level returns in good times and superior, protected returns in bad times. This leads to above-average results over a full cycle with less volatility and helps clients resist selling at cyclical lows.

### 2. INVARIABLE TRUTHS (Market Axioms)

1.  **The Perversity of Risk:** Risk is lowest when risk aversion and fear are high, as assets are priced with a margin of safety. Risk is highest when the widespread belief is that there is no risk, as this encourages carelessness and inflates prices.
2.  **Price Determines Risk:** There is no asset so good that it can’t be risky if overpriced, and few so bad they can’t be safe if bought cheaply enough. The risk of an investment is determined primarily by the price paid, not just the quality of the underlying asset.
3.  **Risk-Taking Doesn't Guarantee Reward:** If riskier investments reliably produced higher returns, they wouldn't actually be riskier. To attract capital, they must offer the *prospect* of higher returns, but there is no guarantee they will materialize.
4.  **The Illusion of Diversification:** In a crisis, correlations between seemingly unrelated assets often converge to one, and everything collapses in unison. True diversification requires understanding how assets will behave in combination under stress.
5.  **The Inevitability of Cycles:** Investor psychology swings like a pendulum between euphoria and depression. This pattern is inviolable, creating cycles of opportunity and danger. Those who forget the past are destined to repeat its mistakes.
6.  **Extrapolation is Futile:** The future will not necessarily be like the past. No value is ever added through simple extrapolation of historical data, especially when it comes to risk.
7.  **The Unseen Nature of Risk:** Risk is hidden and deceptive. An investment's true riskiness is only revealed when it is tested by negative events. In benign environments, even the riskiest assets can appear safe.

### 3. WHAT THEY LOOK FOR (Buy Criteria)

*   **High Risk Aversion in the Market:** The best opportunities arise when fear is widespread, as investors demand generous compensation for bearing risk. This environment leads to conservative assumptions and low prices.
*   **Adequate Risk Premium:** Investments are only made when the prospective return is high enough to more than compensate for the fundamental risk of loss. This "risk premium" is the reward for intelligently bearing uncertainty.
*   **Asymmetry:** Seeks investments where the upside potential is significantly greater than the downside risk. The goal is to structure investments to have a probability distribution biased toward positive outcomes.
*   **Misunderstood or Unconventional Assets:** Opportunities are often found in securities or markets that most institutional investors avoid because they are complex, out of favor, or considered "too risky."
*   **Low Price vs. Intrinsic Value:** The primary focus is on the relationship between price and value. Even low-quality assets can be great investments if purchased at a cheap enough price, as this provides a margin of safety.
*   **Intelligently Accepted Risk:** Willingly invests in assets widely recognized as risky, provided the risk is understood, can be analyzed, is diversifiable, and is well-compensated.

### 4. WHAT THEY AVOID (Red Flags)

*   **Low or Disappearing Risk Premiums:** A market where investors are risk-tolerant and not demanding adequate compensation for risk. This is visible in high valuation multiples and narrow credit spreads.
*   **Widespread Belief That There's No Risk:** This is considered the single most dangerous market condition, as it means assets are not priced to include a margin of safety.
*   **Too Much Money Chasing Too Few Deals:** An environment where a flood of capital leads to deteriorating underwriting standards and a "race to the bottom" in terms of prudence.
*   **Popularity and "Accepted Wisdom":** Avoids assets and trends that are "all the rage" and highly esteemed. Popularity often leads to overvaluation and unknowingly bearing significant risk.
*   **FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out):** Resists the temptation to chase a market that has already boomed, as the risk of participating in the subsequent bust is greater than the risk of missing further gains.
*   **Relying on Simple Metrics:** Avoids equating risk with volatility or relying solely on quantitative models like Value at Risk (VaR), which fail to capture the true potential for permanent capital loss, especially during extreme events.

### 5. POSITION SIZING & PORTFOLIO

*   **Calibrated Posture:** The portfolio's overall posture—the balance between offense (pursuit of gain) and defense (loss prevention)—is calibrated to the market environment. The mantra is "move forward, but with caution," with the emphasis on caution increasing as market risk rises.
*   **Intelligent Diversification:** The goal of diversification is protection against catastrophic error. This requires holding assets that are not highly correlated and will respond differently to the same economic factors.
*   **Inference:** A portfolio is constructed by consciously combining "ownership assets" and "debt assets" to achieve a specific, desired point on the risk/return spectrum.
*   **Strategic Use of "Risky" Assets:** Adding an uncorrelated, high-risk/high-return asset to a portfolio of safer assets can actually reduce the portfolio's overall riskiness through diversification benefits.
*   **Prudent Limit on Illiquidity:** While willing to invest in illiquid assets, this is only done when the return premium is sufficient, and the proportion of the total portfolio committed to illiquids is always prudently limited.
*   **Focus on the Batting Average:** Long-term success comes from making many well-reasoned investments, accepting that some will fail. The key is that the gains from winners significantly outweigh the losses from losers.

### 6. SELL DISCIPLINE

*   **Not a Knee-Jerk Reaction:** A rise in market risk or valuation is a signal to pay closer attention, but not necessarily to sell immediately. The decision to sell or reduce exposure is based on the degree of risk and whether valuations have reached a clear danger zone.
*   **Resist Capitulation:** A core objective is to have the resolve to resist selling assets at cyclical lows during a market panic. The firm's long-term and locked-up capital structure is designed to facilitate this discipline.
*   **Inference:** Selling is driven by a reassessment of the risk/reward proposition. If the price of an asset has risen to a point where it no longer offers a sufficient premium for its inherent risk, it becomes a candidate for sale.

### 7. RISK MANAGEMENT

*   **Risk Control is the #1 Priority:** At Oaktree, risk control is considered "the only thing." The focus is on what could be lost if things go wrong, not just on potential gains.
*   **Qualitative & Judgment-Based:** Risk is viewed as a "concept or a worry, not a number." It is a multi-dimensional, subjective element that cannot be reduced to a single metric like volatility or VaR. Assessment relies on experience, deep asset knowledge, and subjective judgment.
*   **Responsibility of Every Investor:** Risk management is the job of every investment professional, not a separate department of "risk managers." Those closest to the investments are best equipped to assess their risks.
*   **The Two Main Risks:** Investors constantly face two primary risks: the risk of losing money and the risk of missing opportunities. It is possible to eliminate one, but not both. The key is to find the right balance.
*   **Assess the Market's Temperature:** The primary task is to determine where the market stands on the pendulum between risk aversion and risk tolerance. This assessment dictates whether the environment is safe (offering compensation for risk) or dangerous (lacking compensation for risk).
*   **Humility is Essential:** Effective risk control requires humility, a lack of hubris, and an awareness of what you don't know. Overconfidence is one of the greatest and most dangerous traps in investing.

### 8. SIGNATURE PATTERNS

*   **"Second-Level Thinking":** Going beyond the obvious, first-order conclusion to consider the complex second- and third-order effects. Example: A strong economy (good) -> Fed raises rates (bad for bonds/stocks).
*   **The Pendulum:** Constant use of the metaphor of a pendulum to describe the swing of market psychology between euphoria and despair, and between risk tolerance and risk aversion.
*   **"Move Forward, But With Caution":** The firm's default mantra, used to describe a balanced posture in middling markets. The emphasis shifts toward "caution" as market risk increases.
*   **Modified Risk/Return Graph:** Uses a custom-drawn graph showing that as risk increases, the *range* of possible outcomes widens, and the potential for severe negative outcomes grows. This replaces the simplistic "more risk, more return" line.
*   **Insurance Analogy:** Compares Oaktree's credit strategies to the life insurance industry: consciously underwriting a large number of analyzed, diversified, and well-compensated risks (e.g., high-yield bonds), knowing that some will inevitably default.
*   **Memo-Driven Communication:** Dedicates entire investor memos to dissecting a single fundamental concept, such as "Risk," "The Most Important Thing," or "You Can't Predict. You Can Prepare."

### 9. KNOWN BLIND SPOTS & MISTAKES

*   **Equating Quality with Safety:** The common mistake of believing high-quality assets are always safe investments, regardless of price. This ignores the fact that the biggest risk is overpaying.
*   **Over-reliance on Quantitative Models:** The dangerous error of putting too much faith in statistical models like VaR, which are based on historical data and assumptions of "normal circumstances" and fail to account for the extreme events that matter most.
*   **Mistaking the Past for the Future:** A critical error is extrapolating recent performance and assuming a benign environment will continue indefinitely. This leads investors to forget risk just before it materializes.
*   **Career Risk & Conventionality:** Recognizing that many professional investors prioritize failing conventionally over succeeding unconventionally, which leads to herd behavior and prevents them from capturing superior results.
*   **Succumbing to FOMO:** A major behavioral mistake is chasing trends and buying into bubbles for fear of missing out, leading investors to buy assets they don't understand at imprudent prices.
*   **Bearing Risk Unknowingly:** The huge mistake of buying popular, highly esteemed securities that are perceived as having no downside. This is where risk is often most acute because it is least perceived.

### 11. HISTORICAL EXAMPLES

*   **Oaktree's Founding:** The firm was built on investing in assets others considered too risky and out of favor, such as non-investment grade bonds and the debt of bankrupt companies, when they offered attractive risk-adjusted returns.
*   **2008 Global Financial Crisis:** The widespread panic and forced selling during the GFC created a period of extreme risk aversion. This presented a generational opportunity to buy distressed assets at deeply discounted prices for those willing and able to bear risk when others would not.
*   **1998 LTCM Crisis:** Used as an example of the "domino effect" and how correlations go to one in a crisis. Trouble that began in emerging markets spread through unexpected channels to affect U.S. stocks, high-yield bonds, and private equity, demonstrating the failure of seemingly diversified portfolios under stress.
*   **Dot-Com Bubble:** In bubble periods, a disciplined refusal to participate in the speculative mania can lead to underperformance, which Marks views as a "badge of courage." The risk of joining a late-stage boom and participating in the subsequent bust is far greater than the risk of underperforming on the way up.
